# Tariffs—A Double-Edged Sword

In my community, there is a small farmers’ market run by my best friend’s parents, whom my parents know personally. Prices there are always a dollar higher than those at large-scale retailers. Still, we always prefer them to big-box stores like Target because we know our money is going to people we personally trust who are improving our community. This daily dilemma my parents face reflects a broader position the government faces when implementing tariffs: whether to support home-grown businesses or allow cheaper imports to benefit consumers. Since 2024, recent tariff increases have raised the average tariff on many imports, contributing to higher costs across supply chains. While advocates of tariffs argue that they safeguard local jobs and promote self-sufficiency, our country’s past experiences with tariffs make it clear that the trade-offs they entail far outweigh the benefits.

In 1930, the United States government passed the Smoot-Hawley Act, one of the most controversial tariff acts in American history. After the Stock Market Crash of 1929, the American economy entered its largest recorded recession, and lawmakers believed that increasing tariffs would protect American farmers and manufacturers from foreign competition. Despite warnings from over 1,000 economists who argued that the policy could worsen the economic crisis, Herbert Hoover, believing that tariffs would force Americans to buy American goods and help businesses survive the depression, went on to pass the Act. It raised the US tariff on dutiable goods from 40% to 59%, the second-highest in our country’s history. US exports fell from $5.3 billion in 1929 to $1.7 billion in 1933 (Bureau 245).

At face value, this policy could not make the economy any worse, given the state it was already in—but the economists’ warnings proved correct. Tariffs are not a one-sided policy, but rather a global conversation. Canada, which accounted for about 30% of US exports, responded with reciprocal tariffs on 16 products, and many other countries soon followed. As countries raised barriers against one another, global trade declined by almost 66% from 1929 to 1934, and trade with Europe fell by nearly 66% (World Trade Organization 58).

Supporters of tariffs argue that they protect domestic industries, preserve American jobs, and provide the country a strategic bottleneck over trading competitors. By making imported goods more expensive, tariffs encourage people to buy American goods instead of the usually cheaper foreign products. However, reciprocal tariffs destroy any strategic advantage, and under Trump’s 2018 steel tariff, for every American job it created, steel users paid $650,000 more due to higher prices (Hufbauer and Jung). Most economists agree that the benefits of tariffs rarely offset the economic costs consumers face or the retaliatory tariffs from other countries.

Contrary to popular belief, tariffs are not paid by foreign governments (Casey 1). They are paid by domestic companies that import goods, which must pay customs duties. This cost is passed directly on to you and me, who are forced to pay a few extra dollars on the already inflated costs we see today.

Every country has developed its own core competitive parts of the global supply chain that other countries cannot disrupt. Many products rely on global supply chains in which different countries specialize in producing different components. The iPhone, which millions of people buy every year, incorporates parts that come from 43 countries (Petrova). Large businesses depend on global supply chains to produce many modern products, so their costs are dramatically affected by tariffs.

On the other hand, have small American businesses actually been helped by tariff policies? Many small businesses depend on imports to manufacture their products. Lacking the surplus capital to absorb higher duties, even a marginal tariff increase will impact margins and derail their ability to compete in tough markets.

To better understand tariffs, we can look at a more obvious yet essential concept: supply and demand. When the supply of an item is limited, prices rise because the item becomes scarcer and therefore more valuable. When tariffs reduce imports, the supply of goods decreases, creating higher demand for the product. Ultimately, consumers pay this higher price. Furthermore, consumers are forced to make a constrained decision about what to buy because, when supply decreases, the number of available options for an item also falls dramatically. This dilutes the market, giving consumers fewer choices.

Like many others, my favorite shoe brand is Nike, so I was online looking for a pair of Pegasus 41s for track season. It was priced at $145. It struck me that just a year before, I had seen the same pair of shoes for $135. Rising import costs and tariffs on imported goods had contributed to price increases across many consumer products, including athletic footwear.

At first glance, this price increase seems very small. A $10 increase is nothing, given the shoe's price. First off, this was wholesale cost, so in retail, a $20 to $30 price hike would be reflected. Furthermore, this is not an isolated increase, as price surges occur across many industries, and the entire cost of living can rise by $200 to $400 per household per month (Clausing and Lovely 4).

Clearly, history and basic economic principles of supply, demand, and market equilibrium tell us that tariffs rarely produce the benefits their supporters promise. The price is passed on to consumers, and the consumer market is diluted as well. They also destroy trade relationships with other countries. While tariffs may sometimes offer temporary protection to certain industries, their longer-lasting economic repercussions often far outweigh their benefits.

The experience our country went through after the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act shows that tariffs meant to protect a certain group can have widespread consequences and should serve as a cautionary tale today, when tariff policies are on the rise. Just like my parents’ decision at the farmers’ market, economic policy is rarely a simple choice between right and wrong. Every decision involves trade-offs that shape prices, businesses, and opportunities for millions of people.
